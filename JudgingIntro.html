SO, You are interested in learning to be an evaluator (aka: "Judge") for NMRA Celebrations (aka: Contests) and the Achievement Program? Congratulations! Celebrations and the AP would not exist without evaluators. Through your participation as an evaluator, you will learn a lot about what makes an ordinary model something special, and help others do so as well.
 
Evaluators, must undergo training. Training is not difficult, and you will always be paired with an experienced evaluator until you, yourself, have been on several teams. I took my first evaluator training course in 2022 at a Regional Convention and continue to judge when needed, which is at almost every Divisional, Regional or National meet or convention.
 
Your "Guiding Star" as an evaluator is to determine what the modeler tried to do, and how well did they do it. You will collaborate with your evaluator teammates to score the complexity and extent of their build, along with the quality of their build. Together, their skills will result in scores for Construction, Detail, Conformity to Prototype, Fit and Finish, and Scratch Building. Scores over 87.5 points out of a possible 125 will result in "Merit Awards"; Highest Scores will result in Celebration Awards..
 
In 2026, the NMRA is considering revamping its evaluator training, but for now, I use (with permission) the PowerPoint slide decks from Bill Brown (who was the Contest Chairman back in 2022, assisted by Jim Zinser) and Steve Zapytowski (an experienced judge in the MidWest Region) to train judges for the Gateway Division. Together, you and I will explore the links below and study the decks. If you have any questions, click here
 
But before we get into the slides, remember that your job as an Evaluator is to help the Participant improve his or her modeling skills, through advice and support, and your written notes and comments should indeed suggest ways to improve and grow. but always in a positive and collegiate manner. Remember, words can both help and hurt.

Finally, I have included links to several places in the NMRA.ORG web site which contain guidelines for evaluators, forms, and documents for participants in Celebrations and the Achievement program, and these documents give evaluators a good reference for things to consider regarding Structures, Scenery, Rolling Stock and Motive Power complexity and quality (the two axes on the evaluation matrix).
 
General Guidelines - This 10 page document is the heart of evaluator training. It explains the judging matrix.
 
Code of Conduct - Judge with impartiality, recuse when appropriate
 
Rules and Definitions - Self explanatory
 
Forms - If you have ever entered a project for evaluation, you have filled out these forms. Form 901 identifies the modeler, but is not seen by the evaluators, to guaranty anonimity in evaluation. Form 902 allows the modeler to point out information to assist the evaluators in their tasks. There is not much space in Form 902, and modelers ofter provide additional information. HOWEVER, the time available for evaluation is short, and evaluators are not required to digest more information than they can comfortably swallow.
 
Categories - This section describes the 4 categories (Structures, Scenery, Rolling Stock and Motive Power) which require project evaluations: It is a good idea to review these categories's SOQ prior to an evaluation, and especially the "Modeling with the Masters" links at the bottom of each category; which will give examples of what can raise or lower a point score.
 
Evaluator Comments - For modelers, written comments on their model are of great value. But for evaluators, they are difficult to record. In 2025 a form was introduced to make it possible to make quick comments by checking boxes. Written comments are always better, but sometimes there just isn't enough time to do so, or handwriting can be difficult to read. This form may help. Unfortunately, as of this writing, I can't find this form on the web.
 
You need not be a MMR to be an evaluator; you just need to know what makes a model catch your eye with its quality and execution, and be willing to share your observations and thoughts with your teammates and the modeler. Volunteering and Training are the first steps, and thank you for making them.
